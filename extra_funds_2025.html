How to Think About Your Money Choices

Before we dive into the steps, understand that every dollar should be measured by these four things:

  1. Return: How much money will this make you (after taxes)?
  2. Risk: Could you lose this money?
  3. Access: Can you get the money back if you need it?
  4. Flexibility: Does this choice let you seize opportunities later?
The Golden Rule: Get the guaranteed, safe returns first. Then take smart risks with what's left.

Build Your Emergency Fund (3-6 Months of Expenses)

What it is: Money in a savings account that you DON'T touch unless there's a real emergency (job loss, medical issue, car breaks down).

How much:

  • Basic jobs with steady income: 3-4 months of expenses
  • Jobs with variable pay or self-employed: 6-12 months of expenses
  • Have kids or dependents: Add 2-3 months

üí° Example: Why This Matters

Imagine you lose your job during a recession. The stock market is down 30%. If you have no emergency fund, you're forced to sell your investments at a huge loss just to pay rent. But if you have 6 months saved, you can wait for the market to recover while you find a new job.

The "return" on your emergency fund is avoiding forced losses during bad times.

Where to keep it: High-yield savings account (currently paying around 4-5%) or short-term Treasury bills. You want it safe and accessible, not invested in stocks.

Pay Off High-Interest Debt (Credit Cards, Personal Loans)

The rule: Pay off any debt charging more than 6-7% interest before you invest.

Why it's guaranteed money: If you have a credit card charging 20% interest, paying it off gives you a guaranteed 20% return on your money. That's way better than the stock market's average 8-10% return, and it has zero risk!

Get Your Full 401(k) Employer Match

What it is: Many employers will match your 401(k) contributions. For example, they might contribute 50 cents for every dollar you put in, up to 6% of your salary.

Why this is #3: This is literally FREE MONEY. It's an instant 50% or 100% return on your investment, plus tax benefits.

üí° Real Example

You make $60,000/year. Your company matches 50% of your contributions up to 6% of your salary.

  • You contribute: $3,600/year (6% of $60k)
  • Your employer adds: $1,800/year (50% match)
  • Total in your account: $5,400

That's a 50% instant return, before your money even grows through investments!

Action: Contribute at least enough to get the full match. If your company matches up to 6%, contribute 6%. Don't leave this free money on the table.

Max Out Your HSA (If You Have One)

What it is: A HSA is a special savings account for medical expenses. You can only have one if you're on a "high-deductible health plan."

Why it's amazing - the "triple tax advantage":

  1. Money goes in tax-free: Contributions reduce your taxes today
  2. Grows tax-free: You can invest the money and pay no taxes on gains
  3. Comes out tax-free: Withdrawals for medical expenses have no taxes
Pro tip: If you can afford it, pay for current medical expenses out of pocket and let your HSA grow invested for decades. It becomes a "stealth retirement account" with even better tax benefits than a Roth IRA!

Contribution limits (2025):

  • Individual coverage: $4,300
  • Family coverage: $8,550
  • Age 55+: Add $1,000 catch-up
‚ö†Ô∏è Check first: You must have a high-deductible health plan to qualify. Not everyone is eligible.

Max Out Your IRA (Traditional or Roth)

What it is: An IRA is a retirement account you open yourself (not through your employer).

üí° The Backdoor Roth (For High Earners)

If you make too much money to contribute to a Roth IRA directly, you can use a "backdoor" strategy:

  1. Contribute to a Traditional IRA (no income limit)
  2. Immediately convert it to a Roth IRA
  3. Pay taxes on the conversion (but since you just contributed, there's little growth to tax)

This is totally legal and lets high earners get Roth benefits.

Contribute More to Your 401(k)

What it is: After you've gotten your employer match (Step 3), you can contribute even more to your 401(k).

Contribution limit (2025): $23,000/year ($30,500 if you're 50+)

Why now: You've already grabbed the employer match (Step 3), maxed out your HSA (Step 4), and maxed your IRA (Step 5). Now it's time to go back and fill up your 401(k).

Traditional vs. Roth 401(k): Which to Choose?

Traditional 401(k): Choose this if you're in a high tax bracket now and expect lower taxes in retirement. Contributions reduce your taxes today.

Roth 401(k): Choose this if you're young, in a lower tax bracket now, or expect higher taxes later. Pay taxes now, never again.

Not sure? Split it 50/50. This gives you tax diversity‚Äîsome money taxed now, some taxed later. Flexibility is valuable.

The power of time: A 25-year-old who maxes out their 401(k) for 10 years and then stops can end up with more money than someone who starts at 35 and contributes for 30 years. Starting early is that powerful.

Should You Pay Down Your Mortgage Early?

This one depends on your situation. There's no universal right answer‚Äîit's personal.

The Math Test: When Paying Extra Makes Sense

Compare These Two Numbers:

Your Mortgage Rate Expected Investment Return Winner (Usually)
6-7% 8-10% (stocks long-term) Invest instead
5-6% 8-10% Could go either way
3-4% 8-10% Definitely invest instead

But It's Not Just About Math

Reasons TO pay extra on your mortgage:

  • Peace of mind - you sleep better debt-free
  • Guaranteed "return" equal to your interest rate
  • No volatility - your house payment shrinks, period
  • Nearing retirement and want lower expenses
  • Your mortgage rate is high (6%+)

Reasons to invest instead:

  • Your mortgage rate is low (under 4%)
  • You're young with decades to invest
  • You want liquidity and flexibility
  • Historical stock returns beat your mortgage rate
  • You can't access home equity easily if you need it
The middle ground: Do both! Pay a little extra on your mortgage AND invest. You get some psychological benefit and mathematical growth.
Important: Most people take the standard deduction now (thanks to tax law changes), which means your mortgage interest isn't actually reducing your taxes. If that's you, your "effective" mortgage rate is the same as your stated rate.

Invest in a Regular Brokerage Account

What it is: A regular investment account (at Vanguard, Fidelity, Schwab, etc.) where you can buy stocks, bonds, and funds. No special tax benefits, but total flexibility.

Why this comes after retirement accounts: You want to max out tax-advantaged accounts first (401k, IRA, HSA) because they grow faster thanks to tax benefits.

Benefits of Taxable Accounts

  • Access your money anytime: No age restrictions or penalties
  • No contribution limits: Invest as much as you want
  • Flexibility: Use it for anything - house down payment, starting a business, early retirement
  • Tax harvesting: You can strategically sell losing investments to reduce taxes
Smart strategy - "Tax-loss harvesting": If some of your investments go down, you can sell them at a loss to offset gains from other investments. This reduces your tax bill. Then immediately buy something similar to stay invested.

What to Invest In

For most people, a simple mix works best:

  • 80-90% in low-cost index funds (like a total stock market fund or S&P 500 fund)
  • 10-20% in bond funds (for stability)
  • Adjust based on your age and risk tolerance

üí° Tax-Smart Investing

Keep tax-efficient investments (like stock index funds) in your taxable account. They don't generate much taxable income each year.

Keep tax-inefficient investments (like bonds or actively managed funds) in your 401(k) or IRA where taxes don't matter.

Consider a Small Crypto Allocation (Optional)

Notice this is last. You should only consider crypto AFTER you've done everything else on this list.

Why it's optional: Crypto is still risky and volatile. But it's become more legitimate with regulated funds (ETFs) and could add some diversification to your portfolio.

If You Decide to Invest in Crypto

How much: 2-5% of your investable assets MAX

üí° What Does 2-5% Mean?

Let's say you have $100,000 invested:

  • $50,000 in your 401(k)
  • $20,000 in your IRA
  • $30,000 in a taxable brokerage account

2-5% means $2,000-$5,000 in crypto. That's it.

How to invest:

  • Easiest way: Buy Bitcoin or Ethereum ETFs through your regular brokerage (like IBIT or FBTC for Bitcoin)
  • What to buy: Stick to Bitcoin (70-80%) and Ethereum (20-30%)
  • Avoid: Sketchy coins, meme coins, or anything promising crazy returns

Important Rules

  1. Set it and forget it: Don't check it every day. Crypto is volatile.
  2. Rebalance: If crypto grows to 10% of your portfolio, sell some and buy more stocks/bonds to get back to 5%
  3. Only invest what you can afford to lose: Crypto could drop 50% in a bad year
  4. Think long-term: Plan to hold for at least 5-10 years

Putting It All Together: Example with Real Numbers

Let's say you're a married couple making $120,000 combined, and you have an extra $40,000 this year. Here's how to use it:

üìä Your $40,000 Allocation

  1. Emergency fund top-up: $8,000 (now you have 6 months saved)
  2. Pay off credit card: $3,000 (was at 19% interest - gone!)
  3. 401(k) to get full match: $7,200 (both of you contribute 6%)
  4. Max both HSAs: $8,550 (family coverage)
  5. Max both Roth IRAs: $14,000 ($7k each)
  6. Extra 401(k) contributions: $5,000
  7. Brokerage account: $3,250

Total: $40,000

Notice the order: You covered safety (emergency fund), eliminated bad debt, grabbed free money (401k match), maxed tax-advantaged accounts, and THEN invested the rest. That's the power of following the right sequence!

Why This Order Actually Works: The Science Behind the Steps

Each step is ranked by a combination of:

  • Guaranteed returns (debt payoff, employer match)
  • Tax benefits (HSA, IRA, 401k)
  • Risk protection (emergency fund)
  • Long-term growth potential (investing)

Quick Summary of Each Step's Value

  1. Emergency Fund: Prevents catastrophic losses from forced selling during downturns
  2. High-Interest Debt: Guaranteed return equal to the interest rate (often 15-20%!)
  3. 401(k) Match: Instant 50-100% return plus tax benefits
  4. HSA: Triple tax advantage - unbeatable for eligible people
  5. IRA/Roth: Tax-free or tax-deferred growth for decades
  6. Extra 401(k): Continues building tax-advantaged wealth
  7. Mortgage: Guaranteed return, psychological benefit, but opportunity cost
  8. Taxable Account: Maximum flexibility, still grows long-term
  9. Crypto: Potential portfolio enhancer, but only after everything else

Common Questions and Mistakes

‚ùå Mistake #1: Investing Before Building an Emergency Fund

Why it's bad: When emergencies happen, you'll be forced to sell investments at the worst time‚Äîoften during a downturn when everything is down 20-30%.

Fix: Build that 3-6 month cushion first. Boring but essential.

‚ùå Mistake #2: Not Getting the Full Employer Match

Why it's bad: You're literally leaving free money on the table. It's like getting a raise and saying "no thanks."

Fix: Check your 401(k) contribution percentage and make sure you're contributing at least enough to get the full match.

‚ùå Mistake #3: Paying Low-Interest Debt Instead of Investing

Why it's bad: If you have a 3% mortgage and pay it off early instead of investing, you're giving up potential 8-10% investment returns. That's a huge opportunity cost over 20-30 years.

Fix: Use the comparison chart in Step 7. Only prepay mortgages if the math makes sense OR if the psychological benefit is worth it to you.

‚ùå Mistake #4: Buying Too Much Crypto Too Early

Why it's bad: Crypto should be a SMALL, optional part of an already-diversified portfolio. Putting 20-30% in crypto before maxing retirement accounts is extremely risky.

Fix: Follow the steps in order. Crypto comes last for a reason.

‚ùå Mistake #5: Waiting for the "Perfect Time" to Start

Why it's bad: People wait for the market to drop, or for rates to change, or for the "right moment." Meanwhile, years pass and compound growth is lost forever.

Fix: Start now. Time in the market beats timing the market, every single time.

Key Terms You Should Know

401(k): Retirement savings account through your employer. Money goes in before taxes, grows tax-deferred, and you pay taxes when you withdraw in retirement.

Roth 401(k)/IRA: Like regular versions but reversed - you pay taxes now, and withdrawals in retirement are 100% tax-free.

HSA (Health Savings Account): Savings account for medical expenses with triple tax advantages. Only available if you have a high-deductible health plan.

IRA (Individual Retirement Account): Retirement account you open yourself (not through work). Can be Traditional (tax break now) or Roth (tax-free later).

Employer Match: Free money your employer adds to your 401(k) based on how much you contribute.

Tax-Advantaged Account: Any account where the government gives you tax breaks to encourage saving (401k, IRA, HSA).

Taxable Account: Regular brokerage account with no special tax benefits, but no restrictions on when you can access your money.

Index Fund: A mutual fund or ETF that tracks a market index (like the S&P 500). Low fees, diversified, and historically beats most actively managed funds.

Rebalancing: Periodically selling what's grown too much and buying what's shrunk, to maintain your target asset allocation.
